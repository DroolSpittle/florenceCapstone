History of the Chianti Region

History

Chianti is the area in Tuscany south of Florence. The climate in the Chianti region is mild with lush vegetation and fertile soil. Florence gained control of the Chianti region after Emperor Ghibelline and Pope Guelph made a truce that established a border between Siena and Florence. In 1924, 33 wine producers gave birth to the first Association for the Defense of Chianti Wine, the Gallo Nero (Black Rooster). From 1924 to 1967, the consortium had many legal battles to obtain exclusive recognition. This entered the Chianti region into the Denominazione di Origine Controllata (DOC). This also seperated the Chianti Classico from the regular Chianti. Both wines are now Denominazion di Origine Controllata e Garantita (DOCG).



Difference between Chianti and Chianti Classico

Main Grape: Sangiovese
Grape Growing Locations: Florence, Siena, Arezzo, Pisa, Pistoria, and Prato
Percentages of Sangiovese Grapes:
 •Chianti: 70%
 •Chianti Classico: 80%
Amount of White Grape Varietals Allowed:
 •Chainti allows up to 10% white grape varietals.
 •Chianti Classico allows no use of white grape varietals.
Aging Times:
 •Chianti
  •Chianti Normale may be released March 1st of the year following harvest
  •Chianti Riserva requires a minimum 2 years of aging
  •Chianti Superiore also requires an addition .5% alcohol and lowered vineyard yields
 •Chianti Classico
  •Chianti Classico Minimum 12% Alcohol and minimum 1 Year of aging
  •Chianti Classico Riserva Requires 2 years in barrel and 3 months in bottle
  •Chianti Classico Gran Selezione Newest designation as of 2014. Requires 100% estate fruit, at least 30 months in oak barrels
